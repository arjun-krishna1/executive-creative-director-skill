# Voice Examples

Real brand headlines by voice archetype. Use these to calibrate sample_headline output. A great headline is not a tagline -- it is the brand's worldview compressed into one sentence.

## What Makes a Headline Work

Four tests. Every sample_headline must pass all four.

1. **Brand-swap test** -- Replace the brand name. If the headline still works for a competitor, it is too generic. Kill it
2. **Not a description** -- The headline does not describe what the company does. "We make great software" is a fact sheet, not a voice
3. **Not a mission statement** -- The headline does not declare intent. "Our mission is to..." belongs in a pitch deck, not on a billboard
4. **Show, don't declare** -- Voice comes through craft (word choice, rhythm, specificity), not claims. "We're bold and innovative" is a claim. "Don't buy this jacket" is a voice

## Voice Archetypes

Six archetypes with six real examples each. Use these as calibration, not as templates to copy.

### Provocative
Challenges the audience. Creates tension. Takes a position that risks disagreement.

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Nike | "You don't win silver. You lose gold" | Confrontational, uncompromising, competitive | Reframes second place as failure -- only a brand obsessed with winning would say this |
| Patagonia | "Don't buy this jacket" | Defiant, principled, counterintuitive | Anti-consumerism from a consumer brand. The contradiction IS the brand |
| Liquid Death | "Murder your thirst" | Aggressive, absurd, irreverent | Takes the most boring category (water) and makes it feel dangerous |
| Nike | "Someday is not a day of the week" | Direct, impatient, motivational | Eliminates the excuse before you can make it |
| The Economist | "Would you like to sit next to you at dinner?" | Intellectual, sharp, self-aware | Flatters the reader by implying most people aren't interesting enough |
| Diesel | "Be stupid" | Reckless, anti-establishment, celebratory | Inverts the smart/stupid binary. Positions overthinking as the real risk |

### Warm
Welcoming without being soft. Clarity as kindness. Makes complex things feel simple.

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Mailchimp | "Send better email. Sell more stuff" | Plain-spoken, helpful, no-nonsense | Strips email marketing to its honest core. No jargon, no promises |
| Innocent | "Tastes good, does good" | Simple, earnest, cheerful | Four words that capture the entire brand proposition without a single buzzword |
| Oatly | "It's like milk but made for humans" | Conversational, cheeky, matter-of-fact | Reframes the entire dairy debate in language a child could understand |
| Slack | "Where work happens" | Declarative, calm, confident | Doesn't sell features. Claims the space. Three words |
| Basecamp | "Running a business is hard enough without all the complexity" | Empathetic, direct, exhausted-on-your-behalf | Validates the pain before offering the solution |
| Headspace | "Treat your head right" | Gentle, casual, non-clinical | Makes meditation feel like self-care, not spiritual practice |

### Authoritative
Commands attention through certainty. No hedging, no qualifying, no "we believe."

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Apple | "Think different" | Declarative, philosophical, spare | Two words that became a cultural instruction. Grammar-breaking on purpose |
| Stripe | "Financial infrastructure for the internet" | Technical, precise, ambitious | Defines the category rather than competing in it. Infrastructure, not payments |
| Rolls-Royce | "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock" | Factual, understated, supremely confident | Proves quality through a single sensory detail instead of claiming it |
| The Economist | "It's lonely at the top, but at least there's something to read" | Wry, exclusive, intelligent | Assumes the reader is already successful. The magazine is the peer |
| De Beers | "A diamond is forever" | Absolute, timeless, culturally embedded | Invented a social norm. The headline became bigger than the brand |
| Porsche | "There is no substitute" | Final, unapologetic, singular | Closes the conversation. No comparison needed, no competitors acknowledged |

### Playful
Wit over seriousness. Entertainment as brand strategy. Makes you smile before you buy.

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Old Spice | "The man your man could smell like" | Absurd, self-aware, theatrical | Third-person seduction. Talks to women about men in a men's product ad |
| Cards Against Humanity | "A party game for horrible people" | Self-deprecating, inclusive-through-exclusion, dark | The insult is the invitation. You want to be one of the horrible people |
| Dollar Shave Club | "Our blades are f***ing great" | Irreverent, casual, anti-corporate | Swearing as brand strategy. Signals this isn't Gillette |
| Skittles | "Taste the rainbow" | Synesthetic, joyful, nonsensical | Connects color to flavor. Doesn't describe candy -- creates an experience |
| KFC | "FCK" | Self-deprecating, brave, culturally attuned | A chicken restaurant running out of chicken, owning the disaster with one letter swap |
| Ricola | "Who put the swiiiiiss in my cough drop?" | Musical, memorable, culturally specific | Sound as branding. The elongated vowel IS the Swiss Alps |

### Understated
Quiet confidence. Says less, means more. The brand equivalent of a whisper in a room of shouting.

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Muji | "Naturally, naturally. Enough" | Minimal, philosophical, Japanese restraint | Three words that capture an entire design philosophy of sufficiency |
| Aesop | "For attentive skin care" | Precise, unhurried, literary | One adjective does all the positioning. "Attentive" implies both the product and the user |
| COS | "Essentials, refined" | Spare, confident, editorial | Two words. The comma creates the pause. Less is more, literally |
| Braun | "Less, but better" | Reductive, principled, Ramsian | Dieter Rams in four syllables. Not a tagline -- a design manifesto |
| Freitag | "Bags made from used truck tarpaulins" | Factual, material-first, unflinching | No spin. The material is the story. The ugliness is the beauty |
| Snow Peak | "Restore the connection between humans and nature" | Quiet, purposeful, elemental | Not selling camping gear. Selling reconnection. The gear is just the vehicle |

### Urgent
Creates momentum. Time-sensitive, action-oriented. Makes inaction feel like a missed opportunity.

| Brand | Headline | Tone Words | Why It Works |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Tesla | "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" | Grand, mission-driven, impatient | The word "accelerate" does double duty -- speed of cars and speed of change |
| Away | "First-class luggage at a coach price" | Comparative, democratizing, sharp | Borrows the class system of aviation to position a suitcase |
| Coinbase | "The future of money is here" | Present-tense, definitive, FOMO-inducing | Not "coming" -- "here." You're already late |
| Superhuman | "The fastest email experience ever made" | Superlative, bold, specific | Claims the top spot outright. No hedging with "one of the" |
| Notion | "One workspace. Every team" | Compressed, absolute, scalable | Two phrases that answer every objection. How many tools? One. For whom? Everyone |
| Square | "Start selling today" | Imperative, immediate, barrier-removing | Three words. The word "today" eliminates the setup anxiety |

## Anti-Examples

Patterns that feel like headlines but say nothing. If your sample_headline matches any of these structures, rewrite.

| Pattern | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| "Where [X] meets [Y]" | Two nouns colliding is not a point of view. "Where innovation meets tradition" means nothing |
| "Redefining [category]" | Every startup claims this. Redefining means you've already done it -- show the result, don't announce the process |
| "[Verb]ing the future of [thing]" | Vague verb + "future" + category = maximum nothing. "Shaping the future of finance" could be any fintech |
| "Your [thing], reimagined" | "Reimagined" is the laziest word in branding. Reimagined how? Say that instead |
| "Built for [audience]" | Audience targeting is not a headline. It's a brief input |
| "The [adjective] way to [verb]" | Adjective + verb formula. "The smarter way to save" -- which adjective? Which saver? |
| "Empowering [audience] to [verb]" | "Empowering" should be retired from all brand language permanently |
| "Unlock [noun]" | Unlock potential, unlock growth, unlock possibilities. Unlock specificity instead |
| "[Adj]. [Adj]. [Adj]." | The three-word staccato. "Bold. Fresh. Fearless." is not a voice -- it is a mood board |
| "More than just a [category]" | Then say what it IS, not what it's more than |
| "Solutions for [vague need]" | "Solutions" is the word brands use when they can't explain what they do |
| "Experience the difference" | What difference? Compared to what? This is a placeholder, not a headline |
| "Your journey starts here" | "Journey" is a metaphor no one asked for. Especially in industries that don't involve travel |
| "Together, we [verb]" | False intimacy. The brand and the customer are not "together" until the customer decides they are |
| "[Category], simplified" | Simplification is a feature, not a brand. And it's the third most common claim after "innovative" and "passionate" |

## Translation Process

How to get from brand thesis to sample_headline in five steps.

1. **Find the intersection** -- Look at all 3 tone_words from the thesis. Where they overlap is the voice. If the words are "precise, rebellious, minimal" the voice lives where precision and rebellion meet -- controlled disruption
2. **Write from inside the brand** -- Do not describe the brand from outside ("We are innovative"). Speak as the brand would speak to its audience. First person or imperative, not third person
3. **Ground in specifics** -- Replace every abstract noun (innovation, quality, excellence) with a concrete detail from the thesis. What specifically is the brand doing? What does the audience specifically feel?
4. **Brand-swap test** -- Read the headline with a competitor's name. If it survives, the headline is not specific enough. Add the detail that makes it non-transferable
5. **Human filter** -- Would someone who works at this company say this sentence out loud? If it sounds like marketing copy rather than human speech, strip the marketing layer

## Persona Headline Tendencies

Natural voice each persona gravitates toward. Starting positions, not mandates.

### Bierut (The Servant)
- **Voice:** Understated, precise, declarative
- **Archetypes:** Authoritative, Understated
- **Pattern:** Short sentences. Subject-verb-object. No decoration. Trusts the reader to find the depth
- **Would write:** "A diamond is forever" -- not "The most brilliant way to say I love you"

### Scher (The Expressionist)
- **Voice:** Bold, physical, punchy
- **Archetypes:** Provocative, Urgent
- **Pattern:** Imperative mood. Action verbs. Rhythm and repetition. Headlines that feel like they're shouting even in lowercase
- **Would write:** "Don't buy this jacket" -- not "Sustainability starts with mindful consumption"

### Collins (The Futurist)
- **Voice:** Forward-looking, reframing, world-building
- **Archetypes:** Warm, Playful (with strategic edge)
- **Pattern:** Reframes the category. Invites you into a different way of seeing. The headline makes you rethink, not just react
- **Would write:** "It's like milk but made for humans" -- not "Plant-based dairy alternative for health-conscious consumers"
