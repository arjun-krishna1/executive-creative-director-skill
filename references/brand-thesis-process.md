# Brand Thesis Process

The methodology for developing a brand thesis from a client brief.

## Philosophy

The core brief: interview the user, then write a design brief/thesis that allows a junior designer to confidently build a brand book. Make decisions on mood, direction, audience, positioning -- but don't decide colors, fonts, illustration style. Create room for designers to design within a frame that prevents total failure.

The thesis is the creative director's thinking made visible. It should read like a senior creative director sat down, studied the brand deeply, and wrote out everything a designer would need to know to make good decisions without ever having met the client.

## Process Steps

After collecting the 6 intake questions, build the thesis through these 10 sections. Every section serves a specific purpose in the designer's decision-making.

### 1. Critical Requirements

Anchor the thesis in facts. This is the non-negotiable foundation.

- Company name and industry
- What the brand exists to do (one sentence)
- Where the visual system must function (list all touchpoints: apps, packaging, billboards, social, merch, architecture, partnerships, etc.)
- Scale and constraints (how many executions, how many markets, what technical limits)
- What the design must therefore prioritize (e.g., recognition, simplicity, scalability, cultural adaptability)

This section prevents designers from creating beautiful work that can't actually be used.

### 2. Core Brand Thesis

The one-paragraph truth about what this brand really is -- not what it sells.

- McDonald's = "ritual machine for everyday joy"
- Discord = "belonging without performance"
- Royal Caribbean = "adventure platform, not cruise line"

This is the emotional and strategic core. Everything else in the document flows from this paragraph. It should reframe the brand away from its category default and toward its actual emotional territory.

Weave in the founding story here. Why this person started this company matters -- it's the emotional anchor that separates this brand from every competitor solving the same problem.

Include the emotional qualities the brand should always feel (e.g., "confidently humble, playfully optimistic, instantly recognizable, globally simple").

### 3. Audience Psychology

Segment the audience by behavior and motivation, not demographics. Describe what each group wants from the brand. Be imaginative about who these people are and what they already love.

For each segment:
- Give them a name that captures their relationship to the brand
- Describe what they want from the brand (3-5 specific desires)
- Be specific about their emotional needs, not just functional ones

Include a "Fan Truths" or "Emotional Core" subsection when relevant -- small emotional truths about how people actually interact with the brand. These micro-rituals and memories are what make the brand feel personal instead of corporate.

### 4. Competitive Landscape

Map direct competitors AND how they compete visually. This is not a business strategy section -- it's a visual strategy section.

- List primary competitors
- Describe how competitors compete visually (aggressive typography, loud promotional design, mascot characters, etc.)
- Define what this brand should do differently
- End with a clear positioning line: "Where competitors shout, McDonald's should smile"

### 5. Cross-Industry Inspiration

Brands in OTHER industries with similar emotional territory. This prevents category-trapped design. Always include 3-5 reference brands with why they're relevant.

Group them by the quality they represent:
- Cultural icon status (Nike, Coca-Cola, LEGO)
- Everyday ritual brands (Starbucks, Spotify, IKEA)
- Playful cultural brands (Nintendo, Pokemon, Duolingo)

Explain why these brands succeed and how this brand's system should behave similarly.

### 6. Founder/Client Bias Awareness

Name the brands the founder probably wants to copy. Acknowledge the desire is valid but explain why blind copying would hurt them. Reframe toward what actually serves the brand.

Structure:
- "Clients in this category often reference brands such as: [list]"
- "These references reveal a desire for: [what they actually want]"
- "However, blindly copying these aesthetics would [specific harm]"
- "Instead, the system should: [reframe]"
- End with the actual goal, not the borrowed aspiration

Use the founding story to distinguish between genuine brand DNA and borrowed admiration. The brands they admire may not be the brands they should emulate.

### 7. Creative Design Philosophy

Guiding principles for designers. Not rules, but lenses. These become the decision-making framework when a designer is stuck between two options.

Each principle should be:
- A short memorable phrase ("Simplicity Builds Memory," "Emotion Over Product," "Community First")
- Followed by 1-2 sentences of explanation
- Actionable enough that a designer can apply it to any decision

Include 4-6 principles. These should feel specific to this brand, not generic design wisdom.

### 8. Visual System Goals

What the toolkit should include and how the pieces combine into a system.

List the key brand building blocks:
- Logo usage
- Color relationships
- Distinctive typography
- Recognizable composition patterns
- Photography/imagery approach
- Graphic motifs or illustration style

Describe the tension the system must hold (e.g., "consistency and creative flexibility" or "familiar and surprising"). The system should allow thousands of executions while still feeling unmistakably like this brand.

### 9. Narrative/Moments Framework

How design adapts by context. Not all brand moments are equal -- the system should have different modes.

Define 2-4 tiers of brand expression:
- **Everyday Moments** -- The majority of interactions (packaging, menus, app, social posts). Prioritize clarity and familiarity.
- **Brand Moments** -- Campaigns or seasonal activations. More visual storytelling and emotional narrative.
- **Partnership Moments** -- Collaborations with artists, celebrities, or cultural partners. The system may bend slightly while remaining recognizable.

Include tone of voice guidance -- how the brand speaks, not just how it looks.

### 10. Final Direction to Designers

The litmus test. Give designers a set of questions to evaluate their own work:

- "Does this make the brand more recognizable?"
- "Does this feel [core emotional quality] and human?"
- "Does this capture a real [audience truth]?"
- "Does this feel simple and confident?"

Then list the anti-patterns -- if the design begins to feel any of these, it has moved away from the soul of the brand:
- Overly complex
- Trend driven
- Corporate
- Generic
- Detached from human interaction

## Quality Bar

A good thesis passes the "junior designer test":

- Can someone who has never met the client build a brand book from this?
- Does it provide enough direction to prevent bad choices?
- Does it leave enough room for three designers to produce three different systems?
- Does it name the founder's biases without dismissing them?
- Does it include imaginative audience descriptions with cross-brand references?
- Does every section serve a specific purpose in the designer's decision-making?

## Founding Story Integration

The founding story feeds two sections directly:

- **Core Brand Thesis** -- The founding story is the emotional anchor. Why this person started this company is what separates the brand from every competitor solving the same problem.
- **Bias Check** -- The founding story helps separate genuine brand DNA from borrowed admiration. What the founder built vs. what they admire are often different things.
