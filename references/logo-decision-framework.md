# Logo Decision Framework

Contextual inputs that override or inform persona defaults for logo form and treatment. Your persona's instinct is a starting position -- these inputs determine whether that instinct serves the brand.

## Brand Name Analysis

The name itself constrains form. Check before committing.

| Name Type | Example | Form Implications |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 word, short (4 letters or fewer) | Uber, Asos, Zara | Wordmark works -- name is compact enough to be the icon. Lettermark unnecessary |
| 1 word, long (5+ letters) | Patagonia, Mailchimp | Wordmark still viable but needs careful typographic treatment. Consider lockup with symbol for small formats |
| 2 words | Copper Lane, Blue Bottle | Wordmark possible if distinctive. Symbol-wordmark gives flexibility at small sizes. Stacking options matter |
| 3+ words | The North Face, Cards Against Humanity | Pure wordmark struggles at app-icon scale. Symbol or lettermark needed for compact touchpoints |
| Acronym | IBM, BBC, KFC | Lettermark is the natural form. Wordmark only if the full name needs visibility (startup phase) |
| Invented word | Spotify, Figma, Airbnb | Wordmark is powerful -- the name itself is ownable. Symbol optional but can aid recognition at scale |
| Common/generic word | Square, Slack, Medium | Symbol-wordmark recommended. The name alone doesn't distinguish -- the mark must |

## Primary Touchpoints

Where the logo lives most determines what form survives. The 16px-to-billboard test: if it doesn't work at both extremes, the form is wrong.

| Touchpoint | Requirements | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| App icon / favicon | 16-512px square, must read at smallest size | Needs a symbol or compact lettermark. Long wordmarks fail here |
| Social avatar | Circle crop, small size, recognition at thumbnail | Same as app icon. If logo doesn't survive circle crop, create a secondary mark |
| Storefront / signage | Large format, viewed from distance, environmental context | Wordmark shines here. Symbol-wordmark gives flexibility for different sign formats |
| Small packaging | Limited real estate, competing with regulatory text | Compact form essential. Symbol alone may need to carry the brand |
| Large packaging | Full face of box/bag, hero placement | Wordmark or full lockup works. This is where the complete identity lives |
| Print / editorial | Business cards, letterheads, publications | Wordmark or symbol-wordmark. Consider horizontal and stacked variants |
| Merchandise | Embroidery, screen print, heat transfer. Scale varies | Simple forms reproduce better. Fine details get lost in embroidery |
| Vehicle / uniform | Large scale but also small badge. Multiple materials | Needs to work in one color. Complex marks with gradients fail on vinyl and thread |

## Brand Architecture

How many things carry this logo affects form choice.

| Model | Example | Implication |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Single brand, single product | Notion, Stripe | One mark does everything. Optimize for simplicity |
| Single brand, multiple products | Apple, Google | Symbol becomes the constant across product names. Needs to stand alone |
| Endorsed brands | Marriott Bonvoy, FedEx Office | Parent mark needs to coexist with sub-brands. Compact parent symbol + wordmark sub-brands |
| House of brands | P&G, LVMH | Parent rarely seen by consumers. Each brand needs its own full identity |

## Category Conventions

Know the convention before deciding whether to follow or break it.

| Industry | Convention | When breaking it works |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Tech / SaaS | Abstract symbol + geometric sans wordmark | When the brand is human-first, not product-first (Mailchimp, Slack) |
| Finance | Serif wordmark, shield/crest symbol | When targeting younger audience or disrupting (Cash App, Revolut) |
| Fashion | Clean uppercase wordmark, no symbol | When the brand has a strong visual concept beyond clothing (Comme des Garcons heart) |
| Food / Beverage | Illustrative, organic, script elements | When the brand is premium-minimal (Blue Bottle, Aesop-adjacent positioning) |
| Legal / Professional | Serif, ampersand, conservative treatment | When the firm is trying to attract a different kind of client (Atrium, pre-shutdown) |
| Healthcare | Cross/plus symbol, blue/teal palette | When the brand is consumer wellness, not clinical (Headspace, Calm) |

## Form Suitability Matrix

Four primary forms. Each has conditions where it thrives and conditions where it fails.

### Wordmark
- **Best when:** Name is short and distinctive, brand is new and needs name recognition, typography can carry the full brand personality
- **Fails when:** Name is 3+ words, primary touchpoint is app icon, brand needs to work without text (international, iconographic contexts)
- **Real examples that work:** Google, Coca-Cola, Supreme, Zara

### Lettermark
- **Best when:** Name is an acronym, name is long but well-known, need maximum compression
- **Fails when:** Brand is unknown (people won't know what the letters stand for), name is already short
- **Real examples that work:** IBM, HBO, CNN, NASA

### Symbol
- **Best when:** Brand is mature and globally recognized, needs to work across languages, primary touchpoints are small-format, aiming for icon status
- **Fails when:** Brand is new and needs name association, symbol is too generic to own, category is crowded with similar symbols
- **Real examples that work:** Apple, Nike, Target, Twitter/X

### Symbol-Wordmark
- **Best when:** Brand needs flexibility across scales, multiple touchpoints with different size constraints, building toward eventual symbol-only recognition
- **Fails when:** Symbol feels forced or arbitrary, creating complexity without earning it, brand is simple enough for wordmark alone
- **Real examples that work:** Airbnb, Spotify, Slack, Mastercard

## Treatment Selection Guide

After form, treatment determines personality.

| Treatment | Signals | Works For | Fails For |
|-----------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Geometric | Precision, logic, system thinking | Tech, architecture, finance, brands built on structure | Artisan, handmade, organic brands -- feels cold |
| Organic | Human, natural, approachable | Food, wellness, community, sustainability | Enterprise, fintech, brands needing authority |
| Hand-drawn | Craft, authenticity, personality | Boutique, creative, F&B, personal brands | Scale businesses, franchises, anything needing reproduction consistency |
| Typographic | Confidence, simplicity, timelessness | Fashion, media, luxury, brands where the name IS the identity | Brands needing visual shorthand at small sizes |

## Real Examples

### Expected choices done well
- **Google** -- Wordmark. 6-letter name, massive recognition, colors do the personality work
- **Apple** -- Symbol. Most recognized brand on earth. The name and symbol are synonymous
- **IBM** -- Lettermark. International Business Machines is too long. Three letters became iconic through Paul Rand
- **FedEx** -- Wordmark with hidden arrow. Typography doing double duty -- name + visual concept in one
- **Mastercard** -- Symbol evolved from symbol-wordmark. Earned the right to drop the name after decades

### Unexpected choices that worked
- **Airbnb** -- Symbol-wordmark for a 6-letter name that could have been a wordmark. The Belo symbol enabled "belong anywhere" to transcend language
- **Stripe** -- Wordmark for a tech company in an industry of abstract symbols. The name is short, distinctive, and the typography says "we're serious about craft"
- **Slack** -- Symbol-wordmark with a hashtag-derived mark. Unusual for enterprise software. Signaled "this isn't your typical business tool"
- **Glossier** -- Wordmark with a period. Punctuation as brand gesture. The period says "that's it, nothing more needed"
- **Medium** -- Wordmark for a generic English word. Bold choice -- relied on typographic treatment and context to claim the word

Context determined the right answer in every case, not convention.

## Persona Override Rules

Conditions when each persona should deviate from their default instinct.

### Bierut (defaults to wordmark / typographic treatment)
Even Bierut should consider symbol-wordmark when:
- Brand name is 3+ words and needs app-icon presence
- Brand architecture includes sub-brands that need a unifying parent symbol
- Primary touchpoints are app-first or social-first (wordmark alone won't survive)

### Collins (defaults to symbol-wordmark / geometric or organic treatment)
Even Collins should consider pure wordmark when:
- Brand is pre-launch and needs maximum name recognition
- Name is short (4 letters or fewer) and distinctive enough to stand alone
- Adding a symbol would compete with an already expressive name

### Scher (defaults to expressive wordmark / hand-drawn or typographic treatment)
Even Scher should consider geometric treatment when:
- Brand operates in fintech, enterprise, or regulated industries where trust signals matter
- Brand needs franchise-scale reproduction consistency
- Primary audience skews conservative (even if the brand itself is progressive)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Symbol for an unknown brand** -- Nobody knows what your abstract mark means on day one. Earn symbol-only recognition over years, not at launch
2. **Wordmark for a long name on small screens** -- If the name is 3+ words and the primary touchpoint is an app icon, you have a scale problem the wordmark can't solve
3. **Hand-drawn at franchise scale** -- Charming at 1 location, reproduction nightmare at 100. Embroidery and vinyl don't forgive wobbly lines
4. **Following convention without reason** -- "Every tech company has an abstract symbol" is not a reason. Why does THIS brand need one?
5. **Breaking convention without reason** -- Rebellion for its own sake is adolescent. Break the pattern only when the brand story demands it
6. **Designing for today's touchpoints only** -- The brand will outlive its current channels. A logo that works only on Instagram won't survive the next platform shift
