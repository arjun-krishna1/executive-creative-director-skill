# Font Pairing Guide

Proven typography pairings using fonts from `references/font-library.md`. Each pairing is annotated with energy level and persona fit. These are starting points, not constraints -- new pairings are fine if the contrast/complement logic holds.

## How Pairings Work

Good pairings create hierarchy through contrast, not competition. The display font sets the tone, the body font stays out of the way (or in expressive pairings, both fonts contribute personality through controlled tension). Look for:
- **Contrast in structure:** Geometric + humanist, high-contrast serif + low-contrast sans
- **Harmony in proportion:** Matching x-heights, compatible letter widths
- **Clarity in role:** One font leads, one font supports (except in Maximum Contrast pairings)

---

## Restrained

Both fonts quiet. Hierarchy through weight and size alone. The typography serves the content without drawing attention to itself.

### Switzer + Erode
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Swiss clarity meets transitional sharpness. Both fonts are workhorses that let the content lead. The pairing works because Switzer's invisible precision complements Erode's subtle bite -- neither competes for attention. Use Switzer for navigation and UI, Erode for body copy and pull quotes. The tension is barely there, which is the point.

### Manrope + Lora
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Geometric sans meets calligraphy-rooted serif. Manrope's optical size adjustments give it a quiet intelligence at small sizes, while Lora's brushed curves add warmth without drama. Together they produce that "well-edited book" feeling. Lora handles body text, Manrope handles everything structural -- captions, headers, labels.

### Cooper Hewitt + Sorts Mill Goudy
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Museum-grade restraint. Cooper Hewitt was designed for the Smithsonian, Sorts Mill Goudy is a reverent Goudy revival. Both carry institutional weight without feeling heavy. The pairing reads as trustworthy and literate. Best for cultural, educational, or nonprofit contexts where the brand should whisper authority.

### Nacelle + Fanwood
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Humanist softness paired with oldstyle quiet. Nacelle's soft edges echo Fanwood's warm terminals without matching them too closely. The contrast comes from category (sans vs. serif) rather than personality. Works well for editorial layouts that need to feel considered but not designed.

### HK Grotesk + Alegreya
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Open-aperture grotesque plus a serif built for long-form reading. HK Grotesk's friendliness keeps headers approachable, while Alegreya's dynamic rhythm carries long passages without fatigue. A pairing for content-heavy projects -- reports, journals, essay-driven sites -- where readability is the entire brief.

### Junction + Fanwood
**Energy:** Restrained | **Persona:** Bierut
Both fonts belong to the League of Moveable Type family, which gives them shared DNA in proportion and spacing. Junction's clean humanist forms handle navigation and labels; Fanwood's oldstyle authority anchors body text. The pairing feels like it was designed together because, in spirit, it was.

## Balanced

Display font has character, body stays neutral. The display font sets the mood, the body font does the reading work.

### Satoshi + Zodiak
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Collins
Contemporary geometric warmth meets dramatic serif contrast. Satoshi's subtle curves keep it from feeling clinical, while Zodiak's high contrast commands attention in headlines. The structural difference (low-contrast sans vs. high-contrast serif) creates instant hierarchy. Zodiak leads at display sizes, Satoshi handles everything at text size.

### Cabinet Grotesk + Sentient
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Collins
Smooth modern grotesque paired with a serif that has unusual proportions. Cabinet Grotesk's soft terminals and Sentient's contemporary structure both signal "designed recently" without being trendy. The pairing feels curated rather than loud. Good for brands that want to feel current and considered.

### General Sans + Instrument Serif
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Bierut
The most versatile pairing in the library. General Sans has just enough quirk to avoid being boring, while Instrument Serif's fine hairlines bring editorial elegance. Instrument Serif leads in headlines and pull quotes; General Sans does everything else. Works in almost any context, which is both its strength and its limitation.

### Jost + Cormorant
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Bierut
Futura-inspired geometry against Garamond-inspired sharpness. The contrast is extreme -- Jost is circles and straight lines, Cormorant is calligraphic tension and extreme weight variation. But they share a commitment to precision that holds them together. Cormorant at display sizes is arresting; Jost at body size is invisible.

### Space Grotesk + Space Mono
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Collins
Designed as companions. Space Grotesk is the proportional version, Space Mono the fixed-width sibling. Using both creates a tech-native identity system where code and prose share the same visual language. The pairing is almost too easy, but that consistency is its value. Add a serif accent (Spectral works) if it needs a third voice.

### Barlow + Eczar
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Bierut
Slightly rounded grotesk with a multi-script serif that has real personality in bold weights. Barlow stays friendly and approachable in body and UI. Eczar's heavier weights bring unexpected strength to headlines. The contrast in character (mild sans vs. assertive serif) gives this pairing more range than it seems at first glance.

### Supreme + Zodiak
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Bierut
Neo-grotesque sharpness paired with didone drama. Supreme's corporate precision grounds the pairing while Zodiak's contrast and flare give it life. The combination reads as "luxury brand that takes itself seriously." Supreme handles body, navigation, and functional text; Zodiak owns every headline.

### Alpino + Sentient
**Energy:** Balanced | **Persona:** Collins
Soft, rounded grotesque with a contemporary serif. Both fonts feel digitally native -- they were designed for screens, and it shows. Alpino's friendly curves paired with Sentient's unusual proportions create something that reads as Silicon Valley meets design studio. Modern without trying too hard.

## Expressive

Both fonts contribute personality. Controlled tension between two voices that complement rather than compete.

### Syne + Spectral
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Scher
Bold geometric character meets production-grade serif. Syne's heavier weights are full of personality -- angular, assertive, almost aggressive. Spectral, despite being designed for body text, has enough refinement to hold its own alongside Syne's energy. The tension comes from purpose: Syne was made to stand out, Spectral was made to blend in. Using them together forces both into unfamiliar territory.

### Clash Display + Satoshi
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Collins
Variable display sans with editorial presence, grounded by Satoshi's geometric warmth. Clash Display at heavier weights feels like a magazine masthead; Satoshi at text size feels like the articles inside it. Both are Fontshare fonts with compatible geometry, so the transition between display and body feels seamless even though the personality shift is significant.

### Apfel Grotezk + Boska
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Collins
Rounded naivety meets fashion-editorial contrast. Apfel Grotezk's friendly, slightly awkward forms paired with Boska's didone drama create an intentional mismatch -- casual meets haute. The pairing works for brands that want to feel both accessible and aspirational, which is a harder brief than it sounds.

### Anybody + Fraunces
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Scher
Variable width sans meets variable "wonky" serif. Both fonts have axes that allow extreme variation, which means the pairing can range from quiet to chaotic depending on how the variables are set. At default settings, it is already expressive. Push both fonts toward their extremes and the personality multiplies. A pairing for brands that want to play.

### Karrik + Bagnard
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Scher
Irregular hand-drawn sans meets rough-edged handmade serif. Both fonts reject polish, which gives the pairing a zine-like, DIY quality. Neither font pretends to be precise, and that honesty is what holds them together. Use for cultural projects, independent publications, or any context where "undesigned" is the desired aesthetic.

### Plein + Boska
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Collins
Geometric sans with distinctive letterforms (the "a" and "g" are memorable) paired with high-contrast didone energy. Plein's quirks prevent it from disappearing into the background, while Boska's drama ensures the headlines demand attention. Two fonts with opinions, but opinions that don't conflict.

### Chillax + Zodiak
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Collins
Relaxed variable display sans meets dramatic display serif. Chillax's laid-back geometry contrasts with Zodiak's high-contrast tension. The combination feels like a design studio that takes the work seriously but not itself. Chillax for headers and display, Zodiak for pull quotes and accents, or vice versa -- both fonts can lead.

### Violet Sans + Lucette
**Energy:** Expressive | **Persona:** Collins
Monolinear precision meets calligraphic poise. Violet Sans's inktraps give it technical character, while Lucette bridges digital sharpness and calligraphic heritage. Both fonts are refined but unusual -- the kind of typefaces that designers notice. The pairing signals taste without loudness.

## Maximum Contrast

Display font dominates. Body font is pure function. The display font IS the brand, the body font is infrastructure.

### Pilowlava + General Sans
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Organic lava-lamp display against neutral grotesque. Maximum personality in headlines, invisible at body size. Pilowlava's blobby, psychedelic forms are so distinctive that any body font would work, but General Sans is ideal because it genuinely disappears. The contrast is absolute: one font is the entire identity, the other is plumbing.

### Le Murmure + Switzer
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Ultra-condensed whisper-thin display against Swiss invisible body. Le Murmure's extreme compression creates tension and drama at headline scale, while Switzer provides the cleanest possible reading experience below it. The gap between the two fonts is so wide that the hierarchy is never ambiguous. Every headline screams; every paragraph murmurs.

### Basteleur + Switzer
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Expressive calligraphic display (two optical sizes: bold and moonlight) against Swiss clarity. Basteleur's handmade energy and dramatic weight contrast need a body font that stays completely out of the way. Switzer does exactly that. Use Basteleur Bold for primary headlines, Basteleur Moonlight for secondary display, and Switzer for everything else.

### Sprat + Barlow
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Extreme high-contrast spiky serif display paired with a slightly rounded grotesk. Sprat's razor-thin hairlines and aggressive serifs demand attention; Barlow's approachability at body size keeps the overall design from feeling hostile. The contrast works because Sprat is architectural (sharp, structural) while Barlow is humanist (warm, round).

### Terminal Grotesque + Space Mono
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Filled-counter confrontational display plus quirky monospace. Terminal Grotesque's blocked-out counters make it nearly illegible at body size but impossible to ignore at display scale. Space Mono's techy character provides functional text that stays on theme. A pairing for tech-forward brands, events, or publications that want to provoke.

### Melodrama + Satoshi
**Energy:** Maximum Contrast | **Persona:** Scher
Theatrical high-contrast display serif against contemporary geometric warmth. Melodrama lives up to its name -- at large sizes, it is romantic, exaggerated, cinematic. Satoshi's geometric calm is the antidote, keeping body text grounded while headlines soar. The emotional range is enormous: Melodrama for feeling, Satoshi for thinking.

## Anti-Pairings

Combinations that look right on paper but fight in practice. Avoid these.

### Satoshi + Cabinet Grotesk
Two geometric sans-serifs competing for the same role. Both are contemporary, both are warm, both want to be the "nice" font. No hierarchy, no contrast, no tension -- just two fonts agreeing with each other.

### Zodiak + Boska
Two high-contrast serifs canceling each other out. Both are dramatic, both demand display treatment. Using them together is like two lead singers in the same band.

### Clash Display + Tanker
Two display fonts that both want to dominate. Display + display is chaos unless one is used at massive scale and the other at small accent size -- and even then, it is a risk.

### Cormorant + Sorts Mill Goudy
Two oldstyle serifs that are too similar in structure. They don't contrast enough to create hierarchy but aren't identical enough to feel intentional.

### League Gothic + Le Murmure
Two condensed display faces. Both are narrow, both are dramatic, both want headlines. Using both feels like you couldn't decide.
