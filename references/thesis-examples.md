# Brand Thesis Examples

Three examples showing the quality bar for brand theses. These are from the golden dataset -- preserve the voice and structure exactly.

---

## Example 1: McDonald's

### Critical Requirements

**Company:** McDonald's
**Industry:** Quick Service Restaurants / Global Fast Food

The brand exists to deliver fast, familiar, emotionally positive food experiences at global scale.

Its visual identity must function across:
- Storefront architecture
- Packaging
- Mobile apps
- Billboards
- Social media
- Merchandise
- Global campaigns
- Cultural partnerships

The system must work for millions of executions per year across hundreds of markets. Therefore the design must prioritize recognition, simplicity, scalability, and cultural adaptability.

### Core Brand Thesis

McDonald's is not simply a restaurant. It is a ritual machine for everyday joy.

The brand should feel like a shared global memory -- something people recognize instantly, trust instinctively, and revisit throughout their lives.

The visual system must balance two opposing forces:

- **Familiarity** -- Customers must recognize the brand immediately and feel comfort in the consistency.
- **Surprise** -- Small playful variations keep the brand feeling alive and culturally relevant.

This tension between familiar and surprising is the core creative engine of the identity system.

The brand should always feel:
- Confidently humble
- Playfully optimistic
- Instantly recognizable
- Globally simple

The brand is not aspirational luxury. It is accessible delight.

### Fan Truths (Audience Insight Framework)

The brand should be built around small emotional truths about how people interact with McDonald's.

Examples of these truths include:
- The fries taste better when you steal them from a friend's bag.
- Late night McDonald's feels different from daytime McDonald's.
- The smell of fries signals comfort.
- The logo seen from far away feels like relief.

Design should amplify these micro-rituals and memories. This approach allows the brand to feel personal instead of corporate.

### Audience Psychology

The audience is extremely broad. However, design decisions should resonate with three overlapping groups:

**1. Habitual Everyday Customers**
People who visit frequently for convenience and routine.
They want:
- Reliability
- Speed
- Clarity
- Comfort

**2. Cultural Participants**
Younger audiences who engage with brands socially and digitally.
They want:
- Playfulness
- Meme-ability
- Cultural relevance
- Shareable moments

**3. Nostalgic Loyalists**
People with deep childhood associations with the brand.
They want:
- Continuity
- Emotional familiarity
- Reassurance the brand hasn't changed too much

### Competitive Landscape

Primary competitors include:
- Burger King
- Wendy's
- Taco Bell
- KFC
- Chick-fil-A

These brands often compete visually through:
- Aggressive typography
- High-contrast photography
- Loud promotional design
- Mascot characters
- Fast-food category cliches

McDonald's should avoid competing on chaos or loudness. Instead, it should win through:
- Iconic simplicity
- Confidence
- Strong visual assets
- Instantly recognizable composition

Where competitors shout, McDonald's should smile.

### Cross-Industry Inspiration

To avoid category-trapped design, designers should also draw inspiration from brands in adjacent emotional territories.

These may include brands known for:

**Cultural Icon Status:** Nike, Coca-Cola, LEGO

**Everyday Ritual Brands:** Starbucks, Spotify, IKEA

**Playful Cultural Brands:** Nintendo, Pokemon, Duolingo

These brands succeed because they:
- Own iconic visual assets
- Maintain recognizable systems
- Allow playful variation within constraints

The McDonald's system should behave similarly.

### Founder / Client Bias Awareness

Clients in this category often reference brands such as:
- Apple (minimalism)
- MrBeast (high-energy digital spectacle)
- Starface (Gen Z playful aesthetics)

These references reveal a desire for:
- Cultural relevance
- Strong visual identity
- Shareable brand moments

However, blindly copying these aesthetics would weaken McDonald's.

Instead, the system should:
- Maintain iconic brand equity
- Incorporate modern energy in controlled ways
- Prioritize recognition over trendiness

The goal is timeless cultural relevance, not aesthetic trend chasing.

### Creative Design Philosophy

The visual system should follow several guiding principles.

**Simplicity Builds Memory**
Reduce elements whenever possible. Strong brands are remembered through distinctive assets, not complexity.

**Negative Space Is Powerful**
Space should feel intentional, confident, and uncluttered. Minimal composition increases recognition.

**Icons Over Illustration**
Use recognizable visual symbols whenever possible rather than generic imagery.

**Emotion Over Product**
Design should capture how the brand feels, not just what it sells.

**Playful Imperfection**
The brand should not feel sterile or corporate. Small moments of humor, imperfection, or surprise make the brand feel human.

### Visual System Goals

The identity system should provide designers with tools that allow both consistency and creative flexibility.

Key brand building blocks should include:
- Iconic logo usage
- Strong color relationships
- Distinctive typography
- Recognizable composition patterns
- Emotionally resonant photography
- Playful graphic motifs

These elements should combine into a system that allows thousands of executions while still feeling unmistakably McDonald's.

### Moments Framework

Design should adapt based on context. Three tiers of brand expression should exist:

**Everyday Moments**
The majority of brand interactions. Examples: packaging, menus, app experiences, billboards, social posts. Design should prioritize clarity and familiarity.

**Brand Moments**
Campaigns or seasonal activations. Design may introduce more visual storytelling or emotional narrative.

**Partnership Moments**
Collaborations with artists, celebrities, or cultural partners. Design may bend the system slightly while remaining recognizable.

### Tone of Voice

The brand voice should feel like a friend speaking to another friend.

Tone characteristics:
- Warm
- Optimistic
- Witty
- Authentic
- Approachable

Communication should feel conversational rather than corporate.

### Emotional Outcome

The ultimate goal of the visual identity is to create Feel-Good Moments. Every touchpoint should reinforce the feeling that the brand provides small bursts of happiness in everyday life.

Design should leave the viewer feeling:
- Comfort
- Joy
- Familiarity
- Playful surprise

### Final Direction to Designers

When making design decisions, ask:
- Does this make the brand more recognizable?
- Does this feel joyful and human?
- Does this capture a real fan truth?
- Does this feel simple and confident?

If the answer is yes, the design is likely on the right path.

If the design feels:
- Overly complex
- Trend driven
- Corporate
- Generic

Then it likely moves away from the soul of the brand.

## Example 2: Discord

### Critical Requirements

**Company:** Discord
**Industry:** Online communication platform / community infrastructure

Discord is a digital environment where groups of people gather around shared interests. It began with gaming communities but now hosts everything from friend groups to study groups, music communities, developer spaces, and creative collectives.

The brand must communicate that Discord is not simply a messaging tool. It is a place people inhabit together on the internet.

The visual system must work across:
- Mobile apps
- Desktop software
- Streaming overlays
- Social media
- Marketing campaigns
- Physical merchandise
- Developer communities
- Gaming ecosystems

The brand must feel native to the internet, not imported from corporate technology culture.

### Core Brand Thesis

Discord is not designed around broadcasting or performance. It is designed around belonging.

The brand should feel like walking into a lively digital room where people already know each other and are happy you showed up.

The design system should communicate:
- Shared spaces
- Friendship
- Playful chaos
- Digital culture
- Community creation

Where other platforms emphasize audiences, followers, and creators, Discord emphasizes groups and relationships. This brand should feel alive with people, even when only a single visual element is present.

### The Emotional Core

The emotional promise of Discord is belonging without performance.

It should feel like:
- A group chat that never closes
- A late-night voice channel
- Friends hanging out online
- Spontaneous conversations
- Shared inside jokes

The visual system should reinforce the idea that Discord is a living community environment, not a tool. This means the brand must feel:
- Expressive
- Playful
- Slightly chaotic
- Welcoming
- Human

Never sterile or overly polished.

### Audience Psychology

Discord's audience is extremely culturally fluent online. They include:

**Gamers**
The original core audience who expect brands to feel native to gaming culture.

**Internet Communities**
People who build servers around fandoms, hobbies, creativity, or social connection.

**Creators and Developers**
Individuals who run communities around content, projects, and collaboration.

**Friend Groups**
Small private communities that use Discord simply to stay connected.

These users value:
- Authenticity
- Creativity
- Humor
- Participation
- Identity expression

They reject brands that feel corporate or overly controlled.

### Competitor Landscape

Discord sits between several different categories.

**Communication tools:** Slack, Microsoft Teams, Zoom

**Social platforms:** Reddit, Twitter / X, Facebook groups

**Gaming platforms:** Steam, Twitch, Xbox Live

Most competitors emphasize productivity, content distribution, or entertainment consumption. Discord should emphasize shared presence.

Where competitors show:
- Dashboards
- Feeds
- Productivity features

Discord should show:
- People interacting
- Communities forming
- Conversation happening

### Cross-Industry Brand Inspiration

Although Discord exists in the technology sector, inspiration should come from brands that succeed at community identity and cultural energy.

Relevant inspiration could come from:
- LEGO (community creativity)
- Nintendo (playfulness and character)
- Spotify (personal identity through digital platforms)
- Reddit (community-driven culture)
- TikTok (cultural energy)

These brands succeed because their identities feel native to the environments they exist within. Discord should feel like it grew directly out of internet culture, not that it was designed externally.

### Product-Driven Design Logic

Discord's product interface contains many recognizable shapes and visual patterns. These include:
- Circular avatars
- Chat bubbles
- UI panels
- Reaction icons
- Presence indicators
- Stacked conversations

The brand identity should take inspiration from these shapes so that product and marketing feel like one continuous system. The design language should feel as though it emerges from the product itself.

This reinforces the idea that Discord is a place people inhabit, not a brand layered on top of software.

### Founder and Client Bias

Technology founders frequently reference brands such as:
- Apple (minimalism)
- Stripe (developer elegance)
- Notion (clean productivity aesthetics)

However, Discord's audience responds more strongly to brands that feel culturally expressive.

Designers should avoid making Discord feel like:
- A productivity tool
- A corporate SaaS platform
- A minimalist tech brand

Instead, the identity should maintain:
- Internet-native visual energy
- Character
- Playful experimentation

The goal is not aesthetic minimalism but cultural resonance.

### Creative Design Philosophy

The visual identity should follow several guiding principles.

**Community First**
The brand is strongest when it shows groups of people interacting. Design should visually reinforce collective identity rather than individual expression.

**Playful Structure**
The brand should feel energetic and expressive but still organized. Playfulness should exist within a clear system.

**Character and Personality**
Mascots, expressive icons, and visual characters can help give the brand emotional warmth. These elements allow the platform to feel alive.

**Digital Native Composition**
Layouts should feel comfortable inside digital environments such as mobile screens, chat interfaces, and social feeds. The design should look like it belongs on the internet.

**Visual Belonging**
The identity should make people feel like there is room for them in the space.

### Voice and Tone

Discord's voice should feel conversational and imaginative. Rather than describing features directly, the brand often frames communication through the idea of "Imagine a place..." which invites the user into a scenario.

Tone characteristics include:
- Playful
- Relatable
- Imaginative
- Welcoming
- Slightly absurd

Messaging should feel like a friend describing an experience, not a company describing a product.

### Visual System Goals

The identity should provide designers with a flexible toolkit that allows for constant variation without losing recognition.

Key components should include:
- A recognizable logo and icon
- Distinctive color identity
- Expressive typography
- Character-driven illustrations
- Community-inspired patterns
- Layouts that reflect digital conversations

These elements should combine into a system that feels dynamic and participatory rather than static.

### Narrative Framework

Design should reinforce the idea that Discord is a place where people gather.

The brand narrative should emphasize:
- Connection
- Shared humor
- Creativity
- Belonging

Messaging should frequently describe imagined social scenarios, reinforcing the feeling that Discord is an environment rather than a tool.

### Final Creative Guidance

When evaluating design decisions, ask:
- Does this feel like a real community space?
- Does this reflect internet culture rather than corporate design culture?
- Does this feel playful and human?
- Does this show people interacting rather than simply using software?

If the answer is yes, the design direction is likely aligned with the brand.

If the design begins to feel:
- Overly polished
- Overly corporate
- Overly minimal
- Detached from human interaction

Then the design has moved away from the spirit of the brand.

## Example 3: Royal Caribbean

### Core Problem

Cruises are culturally coded as:
- Old
- Slow
- Generic
- For retirees
- Interchangeable across brands

To grow, Royal Caribbean must break the "cruise" category framing.

**Design Goal:** Make Royal Caribbean feel like an adventure platform, not a cruise line.

### Strategic Insight

People don't want cruises. They want experiences worth telling people about. Travel today is identity signaling. People collect moments.

### Brand Position

Royal Caribbean is the most adventurous way to travel the world.

Not luxury. Not relaxation. Adventure. Discovery. Energy.

### Target Psychology

They call them "Seekers."

Not defined by:
- Age
- Income
- Demographics

Defined by curiosity and experience-seeking behavior.

These people:
- Want novelty
- Want stories
- Want shareable moments
- Want "first time doing X"

### Visual Strategy

**1. Icon Brand**
Create a recognizable symbol. The Crown & Anchor becomes the shorthand. Goal: Eventually recognizable like Nike swoosh, Apple logo, Airbnb Belo. The symbol = adventure at sea.

**2. Scale + Spectacle**
Royal Caribbean's advantage is insane ships. Design should communicate: massiveness, engineering marvel, impossible experiences. The ships are basically floating cities / theme parks. Design should treat them like architectural icons.

**3. Emotion > Product**
Don't show: "here is a cruise." Show: a kid flying down a waterslide, someone ziplining over the ocean, cliff jumping, families discovering things. Moments, not infrastructure.

**4. Energy over Luxury**
Avoid luxury cruise tropes: gold, chandeliers, tuxedos, white tablecloths.
Use: bright colors, movement, action, sunlight, water.
The vibe should feel closer to GoPro, Red Bull, travel adventure brands.

**5. "This is Not a Cruise"**
Every piece of creative should reinforce the category break. Subtext: Cruises are boring. Royal Caribbean isn't.

### Narrative Framework

Life is best when filled with adventure. Royal Caribbean exists to unlock unexpected experiences around the world.

The brand promise: More adventure per vacation.

### Design System Translation

From that thesis you get the actual design rules:

| Strategy | Design Result |
|----------|---------------|
| Icon brand | Crown & Anchor emphasized |
| Adventure energy | Bold photography |
| Global discovery | Diverse destinations |
| Big ships | Wide cinematic imagery |
| Emotion | Candid moments |
| Energy | Bright color palette |

### The Hidden Business Strategy

This is actually about escaping price competition. If cruises are interchangeable, people shop by price. If Royal Caribbean is the adventure brand, they can charge more.

### One Sentence Version

Royal Caribbean isn't selling cruises. It's selling epic adventure experiences that happen to occur on ships.
