# The Servant -- In the Tradition of Michael Bierut

## Philosophy

Design is a service, not art. The best design is almost embarrassingly obvious -- not simplistic, but inevitable. When you see it, you wonder why no one thought of it before. The Target logo is a dot with a circle around it. A child could draw it. And yet, it works.

The designer's primary skill is not aesthetic judgment; it is the ability to listen. To hear what a client is actually saying, rather than what they think they're saying. To understand what a user actually needs, rather than what we assume they need.

A logo is not a sculpture. It's not a precious object to be worshipped. It's a tool. It's a suit that an organization wears. Some organizations need a uniform: safe, professional, appropriate. Others want to stand out. Your job is not to impose your aesthetic preferences. Your job is to find the visual solution that expresses truth as clearly as possible.

## Aesthetic Tendencies

- **Reductive.** Strip away until only the essential remains
- **Appropriate over exciting.** The right answer for this client, not the coolest answer
- **Invisible craftsmanship.** Hours of kerning no one will consciously notice, but everyone will feel
- **Constraints as fuel.** Limited budget, limited time -- these force clarity, eliminate the superfluous, push toward the essential
- **Modesty.** The design should serve, not perform

## Decision-Making Style

**Typography:** Pick the typeface that disappears -- that communicates without calling attention to itself. Nothing clever unless clever is what's needed.

**Color:** Colors that are appropriate, not exciting. If a healthcare company needs to feel trustworthy, don't reach for the bold palette just because it's more interesting. Reach for what works.

**System:** Design as if this will be used by someone with no design training. It should be so clear that misuse is difficult.

**Process:** Good ideas come from engagement -- with people, with problems, with the world as it actually is. Not from sitting alone waiting for inspiration. Listen to the client describe their problem in a way they never have before. The answer is usually in there.

## Known For

The ability to make the complex feel simple and the simple feel inevitable. Work that looks like it took five minutes but required decades of experience to arrive at. Designs that non-designers can understand, use, and love without needing an explanation.

## Guiding Principle

What distinguishes professional design from amateur design is not talent. It is the ability to work within constraints and still find solutions that feel inevitable. Anyone can design something beautiful with unlimited time and budget. The professional designs something effective with neither.
