# Anti-Patterns

Banned defaults that prevent generic output. If you find yourself reaching for any of these, stop and think harder.

## Fonts to Avoid

These are what Claude picks when it has no opinion. They are not bad fonts -- they are default fonts. A brand deserves a specific choice, not a safe one.

**Banned as primary font:**
- Montserrat
- Inter
- Roboto
- Open Sans
- Poppins
- Lato
- Raleway
- Nunito
- Source Sans Pro
- Work Sans

These fonts are fine for body text in some contexts. They are never the right answer for a brand's primary typeface because they say nothing about who the brand is.

For distinctive alternatives, see `references/font-library.md`.

## Color Cliches by Industry

If your first instinct matches this list, your palette is category-trapped.

| Industry | Cliche palette | Why it fails |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Tech / SaaS | Blue and white | Every competitor already looks like this |
| Sustainability / Green tech | Green (any shade) | Signals category, not brand |
| Creative / Agency | Purple gradients | Trying to look creative instead of being creative |
| Finance / Fintech | Navy and gold | Borrowed trust instead of earned identity |
| Health / Wellness | Teal and white | Clinical, not human |
| Food / Beverage | Red and yellow | Fast food coding regardless of positioning |
| Luxury | Black and gold | Generic premium signaling |
| Education | Blue and orange | Safe institutional defaults |

**The fix:** Start from the brand's emotional territory, not its industry. A fintech brand about empowerment might use warm coral. A sustainability brand about urgency might use electric orange. The palette should be surprising enough that someone asks "why did you pick that?" -- and your answer should be specific to this brand.

For distinctive palette alternatives organized by emotional territory, see `references/color-palettes.md`.

## Banned Rationale Phrases

If these phrases appear in a persona's rationale, the thinking is too shallow. Replace with something specific to this brand and this thesis.

- "This conveys trust and professionalism"
- "Clean and modern aesthetic"
- "Bold yet approachable"
- "Timeless elegance"
- "Versatile and scalable"
- "Stands out while remaining accessible"
- "Premium feel without being exclusive"
- "Fresh and contemporary"
- "Sophisticated simplicity"
- "Bridges tradition and innovation"

**The fix:** Name the specific thesis section driving the choice. "The founder's background in architecture makes geometric precision feel authentic, not imposed" beats "clean and modern" every time.

## Banned Style Directions

These one-liners could describe any brand. They are not directions -- they are abdications.

- "Minimalist and modern"
- "Bold and playful"
- "Clean and professional"
- "Premium and luxurious"
- "Warm and approachable"
- "Sleek and sophisticated"
- "Fresh and vibrant"
- "Elegant and refined"

**The fix:** A style direction should be specific enough that someone could identify the brand from the description alone. "Architectural precision meets neighborhood warmth -- the visual equivalent of a beautifully designed local bookshop" tells you something. "Clean and modern" tells you nothing.

For real examples of specific, ownable brand elements, see `references/signature-elements.md`.

## The Ultimate Test

**If you could swap the brand name and the output still makes sense, it's too generic.**

Every color, every font, every rationale should be traceable to a specific insight from the brand thesis. If it can't be traced, it's decoration, not design.
